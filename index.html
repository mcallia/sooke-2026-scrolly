Sooke · the road to October 17, 2026

The ground has moved

For a decade, Sooke voted like one of the safest left-of-centre corners of British Columbia — seven in ten ballots, election after election. In 2024 the margin of victory for the centre left NDP was just 23 votes. Scroll — we'll walk the map, poll by poll.

1 · Where you are

One town, three ballots

This is the District of Sooke — T'Sou-ke territory, at the west end of Highway 14, forty-some minutes from Victoria. The dashed line is the municipal boundary; the small dashed enclaves by the river mouth are T'Sou-ke Nation lands, their own jurisdiction.

The colours show what happened to the combined NDP + Green federal vote between 2021 and 2025, poll by poll: teal held on, rust collapsed.

Same houses, same streets — three trips to the ballot box since fall 2024. We'll take it a piece at a time.

2 · How Sooke used to vote

Seven in ten, election after election

From 2013 through 2020, the polls inside this boundary ran about seventy per cent NDP + Green provincially — and the BC Liberals were the only game on the centre-right. No BC Conservative even filed papers in this riding in 2013, 2017 or 2020.

70.0% NDP+Green, Sooke polls, 2013 provincial
85.5% in 2020 (of a COVID-thinned election day)

Federally the same story: 60% NDP+Green across Sooke's polls on election day 2019. (The map's colours stay federal, 2021→2025, throughout — the provincial figures here are town-wide totals.)

3 · April 2025

The wave broke all at once

In the 2025 federal election, the NDP + Green share of Sooke's election-day vote fell to 21.8 per cent — a 29-point drop in a single cycle. The Conservatives took first place in town at 40.1; the Liberals, at 14 per cent here in 2019, took 37.6.

The NDP candidate on that ballot was Sooke's own three-term mayor, Maja Tait.

21.8% NDP+Green, Sooke polls, 2025 federal
40.1% Conservative — first place in town

The map's colours are this change. Riding-wide, Esquimalt–Saanich–Sooke went Liberal.

4 · Where it dropped hardest

Broomhill and the growth belt

The deep-rust patches sit over Broomhill and the newer subdivision streets west of the town core. The poll around Amethyst Way went from 67.6 per cent NDP + Green in 2021 to 25.5 in 2025. Off Eakin Drive, a 40-point fall.

Four years ago these were the strongest left-voting polls in town.

42point drop, Amethyst Way poll (67.6 → 25.5)
40point drop, Eakin Drive poll

This belt is where Sooke's growth is: some of the shift is new arrivals, not just changed minds.

5 · Where it held

Milne's Landing, and the shoreline west of town

The gentlest fall in the district — 14 points — is the poll along Sooke River Road at Milne's Landing, east of the river; it still gave the NDP + Greens 31 per cent. Whiffin Spit and the Grant Road West shoreline held near 28–30; Kaltasin and Glenidle, along the basin, better than average too.

Sunriver Estates itself, across the river, fell 28 points — right at the town average. And "held" is relative: even at Milne's Landing the Conservatives took 44 per cent of the election-day vote.

6 · The provincial echo

2024: the centre-right switched jerseys — and grew

The provincial map moved the same way — with a twist. The BC Liberals, renamed BC United, suspended their campaign weeks before the October 2024 vote and endorsed the Conservatives, handing over the centre-right slot they'd held here for decades.

In Sooke the hand-off came with real growth: the 14 per cent the BC Liberals drew in 2020 became 36.1 for the BC Conservatives — clear of anything the Liberals managed here since 2013 (30 per cent). The NDP + Greens took 63.9, down about 20 points from 2020.

14.2% BC Liberal, Sooke polls, 2020
36.1% BC Conservative, 2024 — the same centre-right slot, 2.5× the vote

The map has switched to the provincial view for this step: since 2020, Elections BC reports local results only by voting place — three buildings for all of Sooke. One caution: 2020 was the BC Liberals' historic floor everywhere, so part of the jump is the old vote coming home.

7 · The whole picture

A different electorate walks in this fall

Municipal ballots carry no party labels — but the people marking them are the ones on this map. The coalition that elected Sooke's councils for a decade has fractured federally and thinned provincially. Its strongest remaining ground is the Milne's Landing corridor and the west shore; the fastest-growing neighbourhoods moved hardest the other way.

And the mayor's chair is open: Maja Tait is not running again.

How to read this before October

This isn't a prediction — it's the ground. The numbers describe who showed up and how they voted; what any of it means for a nonpartisan council race depends on the campaigns run on it.

Election-day vote, n.

Every neighbourhood number in this story counts ballots cast in person on election day — the only kind either Elections BC or Elections Canada reports at the poll level. Advance, mail and absentee ballots are reported riding-wide. Where Sooke's advance vote is visible on its own (2024 and 2025), it moved the same direction by nearly the same margin.

The 2020 provincial election-day sample was unusually small — most ballots that year went by mail. T'Sou-ke Nation lands are outside the municipal boundary and are not included in the Sooke figures. Federal and provincial preference is context for a municipal race, not destiny.

Data & method

Sources.

  • Elections BC — provincial voting results by voting area (2013, 2017, 2020) and by voting place (2024); voting-area boundary sets BS09/BS10/BS11 and the 2024 voting-area/voting-place assignment, via the BC Data Catalogue.
  • Elections Canada — official poll-by-poll results, Esquimalt–Saanich–Sooke, 42nd–45th general elections (2015, 2019, 2021, 2025); polling-division boundary files, 2021 and 2025.
  • District of Sooke municipal boundary — BC Data Catalogue (legally defined administrative areas); shoreline and roads from GeoBC base mapping.

Method. A poll or voting area counts as "Sooke" when at least half of its area falls inside the municipal boundary (tested against each election's own boundaries). All sub-riding shares are election-day ballots only. The 2021→2025 poll pairs on the map are matched by centroid — boundaries are near-identical between those two elections. Two edge polls named "Sooke" by Elections Canada (Silver Spray and the T'Sou-ke-area poll) sit mostly outside the boundary and are excluded; including them changes town-wide shares by less than half a point. Prepared July 2026.
